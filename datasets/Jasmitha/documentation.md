# Project Documentation: Women’s Suffrage in U.S. Newspapers (1900–1920)

## 1. Materials and Objectives
> **What cultural materials are you working with and why?**
>
> This project utilizes historical newspaper archives from **1900–1920** sourced from **Chronicling America**. The goal is to analyze the media's portrayal of the women’s suffrage movement during this pivotal era to understand the linguistic and social framing of the 19th Amendment.

## 2. Methodology
> **What approach did you take (from scratch or auditing)?**
>
> I created this dataset **from scratch**. This involved manual selection, categorization, and metadata extraction for each of the 50+ entries.

## 3. Computational Tools
> **What computational tools did you use to assist your work? How did they help?**
>
> I used a combination of AI and archival tools:
> * **Gemini:** Assisted in generating sophisticated search queries and identifying historical keywords/stances (Pro-Suffrage vs. Anti-Suffrage).
> * **ChatGPT:** Helped accelerate the analysis of article content, specifically in determining the tone and type (News, Editorial, Opinion) of the text.
> * **Chronicling America (Library of Congress):** The primary database for sourcing digitized primary materials.

## 4. Limitations and Challenges
> **What were their limitations?**
>
> The primary limitation was the AI's inability to "browse" the live web accurately. Both models frequently provided **faulty or hallucinated links**, meaning they could not reliably fetch specific pages. Additionally, **OCR (Optical Character Recognition)** quality varied significantly; some older or damaged newspapers were difficult for both the AI and me to parse accurately.
>
> **What challenges did you encounter? How did you address them?**
>
> Because the AI couldn't generate a verified list of 50 links, the process was much more manual than anticipated. I had to copy-paste excerpts into the AI and provide very specific instructions to categorize the text correctly. I addressed this by hand-picking articles based on OCR readability and then using the AI as a "reading assistant" rather than a data generator.

## 5. Curation and Categorization
> **What decisions did you make about what to include, exclude, or how to categorize? Why?**
>
> Inclusion was based on a balance of two factors:
> 1.  **Stance Diversity:** I aimed for a representative split between Pro-Suffrage and Anti-Suffrage viewpoints.
> 2.  **Data Quality:** I prioritized articles with high-quality OCR to ensure the tone and justification were grounded in clear evidence. 
>
> Entries were categorized by **Tone** (PRO, ANTI, NEUTRAL) and **Type** (News, Opinion, Editorial) to distinguish between objective reporting and persuasive writing.

## 6. Emerging Patterns and Tensions
> **What patterns, questions, or tensions emerged from working closely with this data?**
>
> I noticed a higher frequency of **PRO-leaning** articles in my findings. This suggests a few possibilities:
> * **Temporal Bias:** My chosen timeframe (1900–1920) is closer to the final ratification, a period where many states had already shifted toward support.
> * **Search Bias:** My search terms might have inadvertently favored suffragist language.
> * **Institutional Bias:** Certain regions or prominent newspapers indexed in Chronicling America may have been more progressive than the general national sentiment at the time.
